# Lilith Symbol in Astrology: Dark Feminine Power & Astrocartography Guide

The Lilith represents the dark feminine archetype in astrology—the wild, untamed, and often suppressed aspects of feminine power. In astrocartography, Lilith lines mark locations where you'll confront repressed desires, reclaim exiled parts of yourself, and embrace authentic power beyond conventional boundaries.

## Understanding the Lilith Symbol Design

### The Black Moon Lilith Glyph

The ⚸ in astrology typically appears as a stylized crescent or a unique glyph that varies depending on the astrological tradition. The most common representation features a crescent moon shape, often inverted or positioned to suggest the hidden, dark, or shadow aspects of lunar energy.

Unlike planetary symbols that represent physical celestial bodies, the this symbol represents a mathematical point—specifically, the lunar apogee, where the Moon reaches its greatest distance from Earth in its elliptical orbit. This astronomical positioning perfectly mirrors Lilith's symbolic meaning: the point of maximum separation, exile, and distance from conventional norms.

The symbol's design evokes the dark moon, the hidden feminine, and the aspects of lunar energy that remain in shadow. Where the traditional Moon symbol represents nurturing, receptivity, and emotional security, the this glyph represents the wild, untamed, and often rejected aspects of feminine power.

Some astrological traditions use different Lilith symbols to distinguish between Black Moon Lilith (the lunar apogee), Dark Moon Lilith (a hypothetical second moon), and Asteroid Lilith (an actual asteroid). Each carries slightly different symbolic weight, though Black Moon Lilith remains the most widely used in contemporary astrology.

### Astronomical and Mathematical Significance

Black Moon Lilith is not a physical body but a calculated point in space. It marks the apogee of the Moon's orbit—the position where the Moon is farthest from Earth. This point moves through the zodiac, completing a full cycle approximately every nine years.

The elliptical nature of the Moon's orbit creates two focal points: Earth occupies one focus, while the other remains empty. Black Moon Lilith represents this empty focus—a void, an absence, a point of maximum distance and separation. This astronomical reality mirrors the mythological and psychological themes associated with Lilith: exile, rejection, and the void created by suppression.

Astrologers disagree on the precise calculation of Black Moon Lilith. Some use the mean apogee (an averaged position), while others prefer the true apogee (the actual, oscillating position). Most contemporary astrologers follow the mean Black Moon Lilith calculation for consistency, though both methods reveal similar themes.

This mathematical nature—Lilith as a point rather than a body—reflects her symbolic function. She represents what's missing, what's been exiled, what exists in the void created by societal rejection of certain aspects of feminine power and sexuality.

### Mythological Foundation

The Lilith symbol draws its meaning from ancient mythology, particularly Jewish folklore where Lilith appears as Adam's first wife, created equal to him from the same earth. According to legend, Lilith refused to submit to Adam's authority, particularly in sexual matters, asserting her equality and autonomy.

When Lilith refused to lie beneath Adam during intercourse—a position she viewed as subordinate—she spoke the ineffable name of God and flew away from Eden. Adam complained to God, who sent three angels to retrieve her. Lilith refused to return, accepting punishment (the death of 100 of her demon children daily) rather than submit to inequality.

This myth establishes Lilith as the archetype of feminine rebellion, sexual autonomy, and the refusal to accept subordinate roles. She represents the woman who chooses exile over submission, who claims her sexuality and power even when society punishes her for it.

Earlier Mesopotamian and Babylonian texts depict Lilith as a fierce night demon or storm spirit, associated with wind, darkness, and untamed natural forces. Over time, these various Lilith figures merged into the complex archetype used in astrology: the dark feminine who refuses domestication, who embodies raw sexuality and power, and who represents everything patriarchal society has attempted to suppress or demonize in women.
